## **Series 65: Advanced Practice Questions (Form ADV Focus)**

### **Question 1**
An investment adviser has discretionary authority over client accounts and uses a third-party custodian. The adviser's Form ADV Part 2A currently states, "We do not have custody of client assets." If the adviser begins using a standing letter of authorization (SLOA) to direct the custodian to send client funds to a third-party charity on a recurring monthly basis, what must the adviser do?

A. No action is required because the assets remain at the qualified custodian
B. Amend Form ADV Part 1 to indicate custody and comply with the Custody Rule's surprise examination requirements
C. Amend Form ADV Part 2A to disclose the arrangement but no custody exists if proper SLOA procedures are followed
D. Notify the SEC within 48 hours of implementing the SLOA arrangement

**Correct Answer:** C. Amend Form ADV Part 2A to disclose the arrangement but no custody exists if proper SLOA procedures are followed

**Explanation:** Under the SEC's 2017 guidance, standing letters of authorization (SLOAs) that meet specific conditions do not constitute custody. The adviser must: (1) obtain client authorization specifying the third party and amount/formula, (2) ensure the custodian verifies authorization and sends confirmations to the client, and (3) maintain appropriate records. While this doesn't trigger Custody Rule requirements, the arrangement must be disclosed in Form ADV Part 2A as it represents a material conflict of interest and operational practice.

### **Question 2**
A state-registered investment adviser with $95 million in AUM has clients in three states: 40 clients in its home state (State A), 12 clients in State B, and 3 clients in State C. The firm has no office in States B or C and does not advertise there. All out-of-state clients were obtained through referrals. Under the de minimis exemption, in which states must the firm register?

A. State A only
B. States A and B only
C. States A, B, and C
D. The firm must register with the SEC instead

**Correct Answer:** B. States A and B only

**Explanation:** The de minimis exemption allows up to 5 clients in a state within 12 months without registration if the adviser has no place of business there and doesn't hold out to the public. State B (12 clients) exceeds the 5-client limit, requiring registration. State C (3 clients) qualifies for the exemption. The firm must register in its home state (State A) regardless of client count. At $95 million AUM, the firm is below the $100 million federal threshold, so state registration remains appropriate.

### **Question 3**
An investment adviser's Form ADV Part 2A brochure states the firm charges "1% annually on assets under management." The firm actually uses a tiered schedule: 1.25% on the first $500,000, 1% on the next $500,000, and 0.75% above $1 million. A client with $1.5 million is charged according to the tiered schedule. Which statement is CORRECT?

A. The disclosure is adequate because the client was charged appropriately
B. The firm must amend Form ADV immediately to accurately disclose its fee schedule
C. No violation occurred since the client paid less than the advertised rate
D. The firm only needs to disclose its actual fees in client agreements, not Form ADV

**Correct Answer:** B. The firm must amend Form ADV immediately to accurately disclose its fee schedule

**Explanation:** Form ADV Part 2A must accurately and completely describe the adviser's fee structure. Stating "1% annually" when using a tiered schedule is materially misleading. The adviser must promptly amend Form ADV to disclose the actual tiered fee structure and provide updated brochures to clients. This violates both disclosure requirements and anti-fraud provisions of the Investment Advisers Act.

### **Question 4**
An investment adviser representative (IAR) leaves Firm A and joins Firm B. The IAR has a misdemeanor conviction from 8 years ago for driving under the influence. When must this be disclosed on Form ADV?

A. Immediately by Firm B in its next amendment
B. Only if the conviction is investment-related
C. Within 30 days of the IAR joining Firm B
D. It need not be disclosed as it's over 5 years old

**Correct Answer:** A. Immediately by Firm B in its next amendment

**Explanation:** Investment-related misdemeanors and any felony convictions within the past 10 years must be disclosed on Form ADV. DUI convictions are generally reportable regardless of investment relation if they involve false statements or fraud. Firm B must file an amendment to Form ADV Part 1B (DRP) within 30 days of learning of the reportable event.

### **Question 5**
A federally registered investment adviser with $150 million in AUM discovers that its Chief Compliance Officer (CCO) failed to file annual updating amendments for the past two years. Several material changes occurred during this period but were not reported. The firm's first corrective action should be to:

A. File all required amendments immediately and disclose the compliance failure in Form ADV
B. Wait for the SEC to examine the firm before taking any action
C. File a single comprehensive amendment covering the most recent changes only
D. Terminate the CCO and hire new compliance personnel before filing amendments

**Correct Answer:** A. File all required amendments immediately and disclose the compliance failure in Form ADV

**Explanation:** The firm must promptly correct all deficiencies by filing required amendments for all material changes that occurred. The compliance failure itself may be a material disclosure item requiring reporting in Form ADV Part 2A. Immediate correction demonstrates good faith and may mitigate regulatory consequences. Waiting for SEC examination or selectively reporting changes would compound the violations.

### **Question 6**
An investment adviser's Form ADV Part 1 indicates the firm has $105 million in regulatory AUM. The firm is currently state-registered. Based solely on this AUM amount, the adviser:

A. Must transition to SEC registration within 90 days
B. May choose to remain state-registered or register with the SEC
C. Must register with both state and SEC authorities
D. Is prohibited from SEC registration until reaching $110 million

**Correct Answer:** B. May choose to remain state-registered or register with the SEC

**Explanation:** The $100-$110 million range is the "buffer zone" where advisers may choose either state or SEC registration. At $105 million, the adviser has discretion. If AUM reaches $110 million, SEC registration becomes mandatory (unless an exception applies).

### **Question 7**
An investment adviser provides financial planning services and charges hourly fees. The firm's Form ADV Part 2A should include all of the following EXCEPT:

A. The specific hourly rate or range of rates
B. Minimum engagement fees if applicable
C. Biographical information of each staff member providing planning services
D. Whether fees are negotiable

**Correct Answer:** C. Biographical information of each staff member providing planning services

**Explanation:** While Form ADV Part 2B requires biographical information for supervised persons providing investment advice, Part 2A focuses on the firm's services, fees, and conflicts. Specific staff biographies are not required in Part 2A unless they relate to disciplinary history or material conflicts.

### **Question 8**
A state-registered investment adviser with $75 million in AUM begins using a new portfolio management software provided by its custodian at no cost, in exchange for directing $5 million in monthly trading to that custodian. This arrangement must be disclosed in:

A. Form ADV Part 1 only
B. Form ADV Part 2A only
C. Both Part 1 and Part 2A
D. Client agreements only

**Correct Answer:** C. Both Part 1 and Part 2A

**Explanation:** Soft dollar arrangements must be disclosed in Form ADV Part 1 (Item 8) and described in detail in Part 2A. The arrangement creates a conflict of interest (the adviser receives a benefit that may influence trading decisions) and must be fully disclosed to clients.

### **Question 9**
An investment adviser representative is also a licensed insurance agent and receives commissions for insurance products sold to advisory clients. This information must appear in:

A. Form ADV Part 2A only
B. Form ADV Part 2B only
C. Both Part 2A and Part 2B
D. Neither; it's disclosed through insurance licensing

**Correct Answer:** C. Both Part 2A and Part 2B

**Explanation:** The conflict of interest must be disclosed at both the firm level (Part 2A) and the individual level (Part 2B). Clients need to know the dual registration and compensation arrangement, as it may influence recommendations.

### **Question 10**
An investment adviser's assets under management decrease from $105 million to $98 million due to market conditions. The adviser is currently SEC-registered. What registration action is required?

A. Transition to state registration within 30 days
B. Transition to state registration within 180 days
C. File an amendment but may remain SEC-registered
D. No action required; registration status remains unchanged

**Correct Answer:** C. File an amendment but may remain SEC-registered

**Explanation:** Advisers whose AUM falls below $90 million must transition to state registration. At $98 million, the adviser remains above this threshold. However, the AUM change is material and requires an amendment to Form ADV Part 1. The adviser may choose to remain SEC-registered until AUM falls below $90 million.

### **Question 11**
An investment adviser changes its legal name from "ABC Advisors" to "XYZ Wealth Management." The firm must file a Form ADV amendment:

A. Within 30 days
B. Within 90 days
C. At the next annual updating amendment
D. Within 5 business days

**Correct Answer:** A. Within 30 days

**Explanation:** Changes to the adviser's legal name, form of organization, or principal office address require "other-than-annual" amendments within 30 days. This is a material change that makes previously filed information inaccurate.

### **Question 12**
A client complains that an investment adviser's performance advertisement claims "average annual returns of 15% over 5 years" but doesn't disclose that this figure is before deducting advisory fees. This advertisement violates:

A. Only if the returns are not substantiated
B. The requirement to show net-of-fee returns
C. No violation; gross returns may be shown if identified as such
D. Only if the advertisement doesn't include past performance disclaimers

**Correct Answer:** B. The requirement to show net-of-fee returns

**Explanation:** The SEC's Marketing Rule requires that performance results shown in advertisements must be net of fees, unless gross returns are also presented with equal prominence. Showing only gross returns without disclosing they don't reflect fees is misleading.

### **Question 13**
An investment adviser has a wrap fee program where clients pay a single bundled fee for both investment management and brokerage services. This program must be disclosed in:

A. Form ADV Part 1A only
B. Form ADV Part 2A only
C. Both Part 1A and Part 2A
D. Separate SEC Form ADV-W

**Correct Answer:** C. Both Part 1A and Part 2A

**Explanation:** Wrap fee programs require disclosure in both parts of Form ADV. Part 1A requires specific information about wrap programs, and Part 2A must describe the program, fees, conflicts, and other material information for clients.

### **Question 14**
An investment adviser's principal place of business moves from New York to New Jersey. The firm has clients in both states. Registration requirements are:

A. Register in New Jersey only
B. Register in both New York and New Jersey
C. Register in New Jersey and notice file in New York
D. Register in New Jersey and withdraw from New York registration

**Correct Answer:** D. Register in New Jersey and withdraw from New York registration

**Explanation:** The adviser must register in New Jersey (new principal place of business) and withdraw from New York registration. However, if the firm still has more than 5 clients in New York, it may need to register there as well unless qualifying for de minimis exemption.

### **Question 15**
An investment adviser receives directed brokerage from a mutual fund company in exchange for recommending its funds. This arrangement must be disclosed:

A. Only if the adviser receives cash compensation
B. In Form ADV Part 2A and in a separate disclosure document
C. Only to clients who invest in the mutual funds
D. In the adviser's advertising materials only

**Correct Answer:** B. In Form ADV Part 2A and in a separate disclosure document

**Explanation:** Directed brokerage arrangements create material conflicts and must be disclosed in Form ADV Part 2A. Additionally, specific disclosures may be required at the point of sale, especially if the arrangement involves revenue sharing or other compensation.

### **Question 16**
An investment adviser's Form ADV indicates it has custody of client assets because it serves as trustee for several client trusts. To comply with the Custody Rule, the adviser must:

A. Have an independent public accountant conduct surprise examinations
B. Provide quarterly account statements directly to clients
C. Maintain a surety bond of at least $500,000
D. File Form ADV-C within 48 hours of discovering custody

**Correct Answer:** A. Have an independent public accountant conduct surprise examinations

**Explanation:** When an adviser has custody (as trustee), it generally must undergo annual surprise examinations by an independent public accountant. There are exceptions if the trust is subject to annual audit, but for most family trusts, surprise exams are required.

### **Question 17**
An investment adviser representative has a bankruptcy discharge that is 7 years old. This must be disclosed in Form ADV if:

A. The bankruptcy involved more than $50,000
B. The bankruptcy was business-related
C. It occurred within the past 10 years
D. It was a Chapter 7 bankruptcy

**Correct Answer:** C. It occurred within the past 10 years

**Explanation:** Bankruptcy events within the past 10 years must be disclosed on Form ADV, regardless of amount or type. Only personal (not business) bankruptcies are reportable, and they must be disclosed if within the 10-year window.

### **Question 18**
A state-registered investment adviser with $85 million in AUM begins offering a new service: preparing tax returns for clients. This change requires:

A. No Form ADV amendment; it's a separate service
B. Amendment to Form ADV Part 1 only
C. Amendment to Form ADV Part 2A only
D. Amendments to both Part 1 and Part 2A

**Correct Answer:** C. Amendment to Form ADV Part 2A only

**Explanation:** Adding a new service is a material change that requires amendment to Form ADV Part 2A to describe the service, fees, and any conflicts. Part 1 typically doesn't require amendment for new service offerings unless they fundamentally change the nature of the advisory business.

### **Question 19**
An investment adviser's marketing materials state: "Our firm has never been subject to regulatory disciplinary action." In fact, the firm settled an SEC administrative proceeding 8 years ago. This statement violates:

A. Only if clients were harmed by the earlier proceeding
B. The prohibition against false or misleading statements
C. No violation; proceedings over 5 years old need not be disclosed
D. Only if the statement appears in Form ADV

**Correct Answer:** B. The prohibition against false or misleading statements

**Explanation:** This is a false statement that violates anti-fraud provisions. Disciplinary history within the past 10 years must be disclosed in Form ADV, and marketing materials cannot make false claims about clean disciplinary records.

### **Question 20**
An investment adviser with $300 million in AUM uses a solicitors to refer clients. The solicitor receives 25% of first-year fees. To comply with the cash solicitation rule, the adviser must:

A. Pay the solicitor only after the client has been with the firm 90 days
B. Ensure the solicitor provides each client with a disclosure document
C. Register the solicitor as an investment adviser representative
D. All of the above

**Correct Answer:** B. Ensure the solicitor provides each client with a disclosure document

**Explanation:** Rule 206(4)-3 requires: (1) written agreement with solicitor, (2) solicitor provides clients with disclosure document, and (3) adviser receives client acknowledgment. Payment timing isn't specified, and solicitors generally don't need to be registered as IARs unless they provide investment advice.

### **Question 21**
An investment adviser's Form ADV Part 2A fails to disclose that the firm's principals have outside business activities where they serve as directors of publicly traded companies. This omission is:

A. Permissible if the activities don't conflict with advisory services
B. A material omission requiring prompt amendment
C. Only problematic if clients invest in those companies
D. Disclosed through other regulatory filings, not Form ADV

**Correct Answer:** B. A material omission requiring prompt amendment

**Explanation:** Outside business activities that present conflicts must be disclosed in Form ADV Part 2A. Serving as a corporate director creates potential conflicts (insider information, loyalty conflicts) that clients must know about.

### **Question 22**
An investment adviser calculates its regulatory AUM to include:

A. Only discretionary assets
B. Discretionary and non-discretionary assets under continuous supervision
C. All assets for which advice is provided, regardless of frequency
D. Only assets held at the adviser's chosen custodian

**Correct Answer:** B. Discretionary and non-discretionary assets under continuous supervision

**Explanation:** Regulatory AUM includes assets for which the adviser provides continuous and regular supervisory or management services, whether discretionary or non-discretionary. Assets receiving only sporadic advice are generally excluded.

### **Question 23**
An investment adviser changes its fee billing method from quarterly in advance to monthly in arrears. This change requires:

A. Amendment to Form ADV Part 2A and client consent
B. Amendment to Form ADV Part 1 only
C. No amendment; it's an operational change
D. SEC approval before implementation

**Correct Answer:** A. Amendment to Form ADV Part 2A and client consent

**Explanation:** Changes to fee billing methods are material changes requiring Form ADV Part 2A amendment and client consent (since it modifies the advisory agreement). Clients must receive updated brochures before the change takes effect.

### **Question 24**
A state-registered investment adviser with $45 million in AUM merges with another adviser, creating a combined firm with $110 million in AUM. The new firm must:

A. Remain state-registered for one year
B. Register with the SEC within 90 days
C. Choose either state or SEC registration
D. Register with both state and SEC authorities

**Correct Answer:** B. Register with the SEC within 90 days

**Explanation:** When AUM exceeds $110 million, SEC registration is required (subject to certain exceptions). The firm has 90 days from exceeding the threshold to register with the SEC and withdraw from state registration.

### **Question 25**
An investment adviser's brochure supplement (Part 2B) must be delivered to clients:

A. At least 48 hours before entering advisory contract
B. At time of entering advisory contract or shortly after
C. Within 30 days of contract execution
D. Annually with Form ADV updates

**Correct Answer:** B. At time of entering advisory contract or shortly after

**Explanation:** The brochure supplement must be delivered at or before the time the client enters into an advisory contract with the supervised person. In practice, many advisers deliver it with the Part 2A brochure.

### **Question 26**
An investment adviser includes hypothetical performance in its marketing materials. Under the Marketing Rule, the adviser must:

A. Obtain prior written consent from the SEC
B. Maintain records supporting all hypothetical assumptions
C. Ensure the performance is net of all fees and expenses
D. Both B and C

**Correct Answer:** D. Both B and C

**Explanation:** For hypothetical performance, the Marketing Rule requires: (1) adopting policies to ensure performance is relevant to likely financial situation/investment objectives, (2) providing information on assumptions, (3) disclosing limitations, and (4) maintaining records. Net performance presentation is generally required for all performance advertising.

### **Question 27**
An investment adviser has a website that states: "Our investment strategies have consistently outperformed the market." This statement is:

A. Permissible if accompanied by past performance data
B. Permissible if accompanied by "past performance" disclaimer
C. Prohibited as an unsubstantiated claim
D. Permissible for institutional clients only

**Correct Answer:** C. Prohibited as an unsubstantiated claim

**Explanation:** Unsubstantiated claims of outperformance are prohibited as misleading. Even with disclaimers, such absolute claims violate anti-fraud rules unless they can be substantiated with specific, accurate performance data presented in compliance with Marketing Rule requirements.

### **Question 28**
An investment adviser's compliance officer discovers that the firm has been calculating regulatory AUM incorrectly by excluding non-discretionary managed accounts. The corrected AUM is $115 million, while the firm reported $95 million. The firm is state-registered. The first corrective step is to:

A. File an amendment to correct AUM and transition to SEC registration
B. Reduce AUM to stay below $100 million threshold
C. File an amendment but remain state-registered
D. Notify clients but take no regulatory action

**Correct Answer:** A. File an amendment to correct AUM and transition to SEC registration

**Explanation:** At $115 million AUM, the firm exceeds the $110 million mandatory SEC registration threshold. The firm must file an amendment to correct the AUM and begin the process of registering with the SEC and withdrawing from state registration.

### **Question 29**
An investment adviser's Form ADV indicates it participates in wrap fee programs. In its brochure, the adviser must disclose:

A. Only the total wrap fee percentage
B. The wrap fee and underlying expenses
C. That wrap fees may be higher than separate fees
D. Both B and C

**Correct Answer:** D. Both B and C

**Explanation:** For wrap fee programs, Form ADV Part 2A must disclose both the wrap fee itself and the fact that underlying investments have their own expenses. The brochure should also disclose that clients might pay more in a wrap program than if they paid separately for advice and execution.

### **Question 30**
An investment adviser representative receives gifts from a broker-dealer totaling $350 in a year. This must be disclosed:

A. In Form ADV Part 1
B. In Form ADV Part 2A
C. In both Part 1 and Part 2A
D. Only if gifts exceed $100 per instance

**Correct Answer:** C. In both Part 1 and Part 2A

**Explanation:** Gifts or gratuities from third parties that relate to advisory services create conflicts and must be disclosed. Form ADV Part 1 asks about receipt of non-cash compensation. Part 2A should disclose the conflict generally, though specific dollar amounts may not be required.

### **Question 31**
An investment adviser's annual updating amendment to Form ADV must be filed within:

A. 30 days of fiscal year end
B. 60 days of fiscal year end
C. 90 days of fiscal year end
D. 120 days of fiscal year end

**Correct Answer:** C. 90 days of fiscal year end

**Explanation:** Form ADV annual updating amendments are due within 90 days of the adviser's fiscal year end. This updates all information and renews the registration.

### **Question 32**
An investment adviser uses social media to communicate with clients. A representative posts: "Just recommended ABC stock to all my clients - expect big gains!" This communication:

A. Violates restrictions on specific investment recommendations
B. Is permissible if only clients see the post
C. Is permissible with past performance disclaimer
D. Violates confidentiality but not advertising rules

**Correct Answer:** A. Violates restrictions on specific investment recommendations

**Explanation:** Specific investment recommendations on social media may violate advertising rules if they: (1) aren't fair and balanced, (2) make unsubstantiated claims, (3) don't consider appropriateness for all recipients, or (4) violate recordkeeping requirements. Such posts also raise suitability and fiduciary duty concerns.

### **Question 33**
An investment adviser has custody because it serves as trustee. The adviser must ensure clients receive account statements:

A. Directly from the adviser quarterly
B. Directly from the custodian quarterly
C. Directly from the custodian monthly
D. From either the adviser or custodian quarterly

**Correct Answer:** B. Directly from the custodian quarterly

**Explanation:** When an adviser has custody, qualified custodians must send account statements directly to clients at least quarterly. This independent verification helps protect client assets.

### **Question 34**
An investment adviser's brochure states it "may invest client assets in mutual funds." The firm actually invests almost exclusively in proprietary mutual funds. This disclosure is:

A. Adequate; proprietary funds are mutual funds
B. Inadequate; it omits material conflict information
C. Adequate if disclosed in client agreements
D. Inadequate only for non-accredited investors

**Correct Answer:** B. Inadequate; it omits material conflict information

**Explanation:** Investing in proprietary funds creates significant conflicts (the adviser earns management fees from the funds). This must be specifically disclosed in Form ADV Part 2A, including how the conflict is addressed.

### **Question 35**
An investment adviser's chief compliance officer leaves the firm. This change requires a Form ADV amendment:

A. Within 30 days
B. Within 90 days
C. At next annual update

**Correct Answer:** A. Within 30 days

**Explanation:** Changes in executive officers, including the CCO, are material changes requiring "other-than-annual" amendments within 30 days. The CCO is typically listed in Form ADV Schedule A.

### **Question 36**
An investment adviser's Form ADV Part 1 indicates the firm has 6 non-clerical employees. The firm hires 2 new investment adviser representatives. The amendment to update this information must be filed:

A. Within 30 days
B. Within 90 days
C. At next annual update
D. Within 5 business days

**Correct Answer:** A. Within 30 days

**Explanation:** Changes in the number of employees, particularly IARs, are material changes requiring prompt amendment. The firm must also file Form U4 for each new IAR and update Schedule A of Form ADV.

### **Question 37**
An investment adviser receives research from a broker-dealer in exchange for directing client trades. Under Section 28(e), this soft dollar arrangement:

A. Must provide research that assists in investment decisions
B. May include general office equipment
C. Must be disclosed only if research exceeds 5% of commissions
D. Is prohibited for ERISA accounts

**Correct Answer:** A. Must provide research that assists in investment decisions

**Explanation:** Section 28(e) safe harbor permits paying more than lowest commission only for "brokerage and research services" that provide lawful and appropriate assistance. General office equipment or non-research services don't qualify.

### **Question 38**
An investment adviser's performance advertisement shows 1, 5, and 10-year returns. Under the Marketing Rule, the advertisement must also show:

A. The performance of a relevant benchmark
B. Each individual client's actual returns
C. The worst-performing account returns
D. Performance gross of all fees and expenses

**Correct Answer:** A. The performance of a relevant benchmark

**Explanation:** The Marketing Rule requires that performance presentations include or offer to provide performance of an appropriate benchmark, unless the advertisement doesn't show performance over time periods. Benchmarks must be comparable to the advertised strategy.

An investment adviser's brochure supplement for an IAR must include:

B. Details of the IAR's educational background
C. The IAR's exact assets under management
D. All business activities outside the firm

**Correct Answer:** B. Details of the IAR's educational background

**Explanation:** Form ADV Part 2B requires: educational background and business experience, disciplinary history, other business activities, additional compensation, and supervision. Specific compensation details and exact AUM are not required.

### **Question 40**
An investment adviser with $500 million in AUM changes its state of incorporation from Delaware to Nevada. This requires:

B. New registration with the SEC
C. Consent from all existing clients
D. Both A and B

**Correct Answer:** A. Amendment to Form ADV Part 1 within 30 days

**Explanation:** Changes in legal organization or state of incorporation require Form ADV amendments within 30 days. Since the firm remains SEC-registered (well above thresholds), no new SEC registration is needed, though state notice filings may be required.

### **Question 41**
An investment adviser's marketing materials include testimonials from three clients. Under the Marketing Rule, the adviser must:

A. Disclose whether testimonial providers were compensated
B. Obtain prior SEC approval for the testimonials
C. Ensure testimonials represent average client experience
D. All of the above

**Correct Answer:** A. Disclose whether testimonial providers were compensated

**Explanation:** The Marketing Rule permits testimonials if specific disclosures are made, including whether providers received compensation and any material conflicts. SEC approval is not required, and testimonials need not represent average experience but must not be misleading.

### **Question 42**
An investment adviser discovers an error in its Form ADV Part 2A regarding the description of investment strategies. The brochure stated "we do not use options" when in fact some client accounts use options for hedging. The firm must:

A. Correct the error in the next annual amendment
B. Amend immediately and provide corrected brochures to clients
C. Notify clients but wait for annual update to amend
D. Only correct for new clients going forward

**Correct Answer:** B. Amend immediately and provide corrected brochures to clients

**Explanation:** Material inaccuracies in Part 2A require prompt correction. The firm must amend Form ADV and deliver updated brochures to existing clients. This is not a matter that can wait for the annual update.

### **Question 43**
An investment adviser's Form ADV Part 1 indicates the firm has 120 clients. The adviser determines that 15 of these are family members who receive services at no cost. For Form ADV purposes, these family members:

A. Must be counted as clients
B. Should not be counted as clients
C. Count as half a client each
D. Count only if they have over $50,000 in assets

**Correct Answer:** B. Should not be counted as clients

**Explanation:** Family members who receive advisory services without compensation and as an accommodation generally should not be counted as clients for Form ADV purposes. The test is whether there is a bona fide advisory relationship with compensation.

### **Question 44**
An investment adviser uses model portfolios that are occasionally adjusted. The firm must disclose in Form ADV Part 2A:

A. Each specific security in the models
B. How often models are reviewed and rebalanced
C. Past performance of each model portfolio
D. The exact number of clients in each model

**Correct Answer:** B. How often models are reviewed and rebalanced

**Explanation:** Form ADV Part 2A should describe investment strategies, including use of models, frequency of review, and rebalancing approach. Specific securities, past performance, and client counts are not required in the brochure.

### **Question 45**
An investment adviser has a Code of Ethics that requires reporting of personal securities transactions. Under the Code, access persons must report transactions:

A. Within 30 days of trade date
B. Within 10 days of trade date
C. Within 5 days of trade date
D. Within 48 hours of trade date

**Correct Answer:** B. Within 10 days of trade date

**Explanation:** Rule 204A-1 requires access persons to report personal securities transactions at least quarterly, with transactions reported no later than 30 days after quarter end. Many firms require more frequent reporting, but the rule minimum is quarterly with 30-day reporting.

### **Question 46**
An investment adviser's Form ADV indicates it has $80 million in regulatory AUM. The firm has a separate $25 million hedge fund it advises. For Form ADV purposes, the hedge fund:

A. Counts as one client
B. Counts each investor as a client
C. Is excluded from AUM calculations
D. Is excluded from client count but included in AUM

**Correct Answer:** A. Counts as one client

**Explanation:** Private funds are generally counted as one client for Form ADV purposes (the fund itself), but the fund's assets are included in regulatory AUM. This prevents double-counting of investors while accurately reflecting assets managed.

### **Question 47**
An investment adviser changes its custody arrangement from having a related person as custodian to using an independent third-party custodian. This change requires:

A. Amendment to Form ADV within 30 days
B. Client consent for each account
C. SEC approval of the new custodian
D. Both A and B

**Correct Answer:** A. Amendment to Form ADV within 30 days

**Explanation:** Changes in custody arrangements are material changes requiring Form ADV amendment within 30 days. Client consent may be required if the advisory agreement specifies the custodian, but regulatory approval of the custodian is not generally required.

### **Question 48**

A. Within 30 days
B. Within 90 days

**Correct Answer:** A. Within 30 days

### **Question 50**
An investment adviser with $200 million in AUM merges with another adviser. Post-merger, the combined firm must file:

A. An amendment to existing Form ADV
B. A new Form ADV registration
C. Form ADV-W for the disappearing firm
D. Both B and C

**Correct Answer:** D. Both B and C

**Explanation:** In a merger, the surviving firm typically files an amendment to its Form ADV, while the disappearing firm files Form ADV-W (withdrawal). However, if the merger creates what is effectively a new entity, a new registration may be required. The specific approach depends on the merger structure and regulatory guidance.

## Question 1
Bright Future Advisers, LLC is a state-registered investment adviser with $85 million in assets under management. The firm currently has 12 investment adviser representatives and provides portfolio management services to approximately 180 clients, including individuals, small businesses, and trusts. The firm's Chief Compliance Officer, Jennifer, is conducting the annual review of the firm's Form ADV and discovers several issues: (1) The firm began offering financial planning services 8 months ago but never updated Part 2A to reflect this new service; (2) Two IARs left the firm 5 months ago, but the firm never filed amendments to remove them from Form ADV Part 1; (3) The firm's assets under management have grown to $92 million, crossing potential federal registration thresholds, but this hasn't been updated; (4) The principal office address changed 3 months ago when the firm relocated, but Form ADV still lists the old address; and (5) A disciplinary action against one of the firm's IARs was resolved 4 months ago, but this disclosure was never added. How should Jennifer address these Form ADV compliance issues, and what are the potential regulatory consequences?

A. Jennifer should immediately file Form ADV amendments for all material changes, recognizing that the firm has violated the requirement to file "other-than-annual" amendments within 30 days of material changes, and the firm may face regulatory sanctions for these failures
B. Jennifer can wait until the next annual updating amendment is due since all changes occurred within the same fiscal year and no clients have specifically requested updated disclosure documents
C. Jennifer only needs to file amendments for items (2) and (4) since changes in personnel and address are the only items requiring prompt amendment; service offerings and AUM can be updated annually
D. Jennifer should file a complete new Form ADV registration rather than amendments, since multiple sections require updates and this is the most efficient approach

**Correct Answer:** A. Jennifer should immediately file Form ADV amendments for all material changes, recognizing that the firm has violated the requirement to file "other-than-annual" amendments within 30 days of material changes, and the firm may face regulatory sanctions for these failures

**Explanation:** Investment advisers must file "other-than-annual amendments" to Form ADV within 30 days whenever information in Form ADV Part 1 becomes inaccurate in any way, and must update Part 2 (the brochure) promptly when information becomes materially inaccurate. Bright Future Advisers has violated these requirements in multiple ways: (1) Adding financial planning services is a material change requiring prompt amendment to Part 2A; (2) Changes in IARs must be reflected in Form ADV Part 1 within 30 days; (3) AUM growth that potentially triggers federal registration requirements is material and requires prompt updating; (4) Principal office address changes must be updated within 30 days; (5) Disciplinary events must be disclosed promptly. The firm has been operating with an inaccurate Form ADV for months, which is a serious compliance violation. Option B is incorrect because material changes cannot wait for the annual update - they require prompt "other-than-annual amendments" within 30 days. Option C is incorrect because all five items listed are material changes requiring prompt amendment, not just personnel and address changes. Option D is incorrect because amendments are the proper mechanism for updates; filing an entirely new registration is unnecessary and inappropriate for routine updates.

## Question 2
Greenfield Investment Management is a federally registered investment adviser with $450 million in assets under management. The firm primarily serves high-net-worth individuals and maintains full discretionary authority over most client accounts. During the annual Form ADV update process, the compliance team is reviewing whether certain arrangements and practices must be disclosed in Part 2A (the firm brochure). The following situations have arisen in the past year: (1) The firm has a soft dollar arrangement with its primary broker-dealer where the firm receives research reports and portfolio analysis software in exchange for directing client trades to that broker, potentially resulting in higher commission costs for clients; (2) The firm's founding partner owns 15% equity interest in a private hedge fund, and the firm occasionally recommends this hedge fund to qualified clients when appropriate for their objectives; (3) The firm uses a third-party portfolio accounting system provided by a vendor that also provides custodial services, and the firm receives a 20% discount on the accounting software when clients use that vendor as custodian; (4) One of the firm's senior advisers is also a licensed insurance agent and occasionally recommends insurance products to clients, earning commissions on those sales; (5) The firm has an informal referral arrangement with three local CPA firms where they refer clients to each other, though no cash compensation is exchanged. Which of these arrangements MUST be disclosed in Form ADV Part 2A under current SEC requirements?

A. Only items (1), (2), and (4) require disclosure because they involve direct financial compensation or conflicts of interest
B. Items (1), (2), (3), (4), and (5) all require disclosure because each represents a conflict of interest or arrangement that could influence the adviser's recommendations
C. Only items (1) and (2) require disclosure because soft dollars and affiliated investments are the primary regulatory concerns; the other items are immaterial business practices
D. Only items (1), (2), and (5) require disclosure; item (3) is a normal business arrangement and item (4) is covered by the insurance agent's separate licensing disclosure

**Correct Answer:** B. Items (1), (2), (3), (4), and (5) all require disclosure because each represents a conflict of interest or arrangement that could influence the adviser's recommendations

**Explanation:** Form ADV Part 2A requires comprehensive disclosure of all conflicts of interest and arrangements that could influence the adviser's advice or create incentives that may not align with client interests. Each of these situations must be disclosed: (1) Soft dollar arrangements MUST be disclosed in detail, including what benefits the firm receives and how this might affect commission costs - this is a significant conflict because the firm benefits from directing trades to a particular broker potentially at higher cost to clients; (2) Any financial interest in a recommended investment (even 15% of a hedge fund) is a material conflict that must be disclosed - clients need to know the adviser has a financial stake in recommended investments; (3) The 20% discount on software creates an incentive to recommend a particular custodian, which is a conflict of interest requiring disclosure even though it may seem like a normal business arrangement; (4) Dual registration as an insurance agent and receipt of insurance commissions is a classic conflict that MUST be disclosed - clients need to know the adviser receives additional compensation for insurance recommendations; (5) Referral arrangements, even informal ones without cash compensation, must be disclosed because they create reciprocal incentives that could influence advice.

## Question 3
Horizon Wealth Advisers is a state-registered investment adviser that has experienced significant growth. Three years ago, the firm had $28 million in AUM and 45 clients. Today, the firm has $118 million in AUM and 156 clients spread across multiple states. The firm's principals, David and Sarah, are meeting with their compliance consultant to discuss their registration obligations. The compliance consultant informs them of the following: (1) They may now be required to register with the SEC as a federal covered adviser because they've exceeded $110 million in AUM; (2) They are currently registered only in their home state (State A) but have clients in five other states (States B, C, D, E, and F); (3) In State B, they have 8 clients; in State C, they have 12 clients; in State D, they have 4 clients; in State E, they have 2 clients; and in State F, they have 6 clients; (4) None of these clients were solicited through advertising or general solicitation - all came through referrals or personal networks; (5) The firm maintains no offices outside of State A. Given these facts and applying the de minimis exemption and federal/state registration rules, what are Horizon's registration obligations?

A. Horizon must register with the SEC and terminate all state registrations because they exceed $110 million AUM and federal registration preempts state registration requirements
B. Horizon must register with the SEC (due to exceeding $110 million AUM), must maintain registration in State A (where they have their principal office), and must register in States B, C, and F (where they have more than 5 clients), but can rely on the de minimis exemption in States D and E
C. Horizon should remain state-registered in State A only until they reach $120 million AUM (the mandatory federal registration threshold) and need not register in States B-F because they have no offices in those states and all clients came through referrals
D. Horizon must register with the SEC, must register in all six states where they have clients (A, B, C, D, E, F) because the de minimis exemption does not apply once a firm becomes federally registered, and must file Form ADV with both the SEC and each state

**Correct Answer:** B. Horizon must register with the SEC (due to exceeding $110 million AUM), must maintain registration in State A (where they have their principal office), and must register in States B, C, and F (where they have more than 5 clients), but can rely on the de minimis exemption in States D and E

**Explanation:** This question tests understanding of the federal/state registration thresholds and the de minimis exemption. Investment advisers with AUM of $110 million to $120 million MAY register with the SEC (permissive range), while those with $120 million or more MUST register. At $118 million, Horizon is in the permissive range and should register with the SEC. Even federal covered advisers must maintain notice filings in states where they have their principal place of business. The de minimis exemption allows advisers to have up to 5 clients in a state during a 12-month period without registering, provided: (a) the adviser has no office in that state, and (b) the adviser does not hold itself out to the public as an investment adviser. State B (8 clients), State C (12 clients), and State F (6 clients) exceed the 5-client threshold, requiring notice filing. States D (4 clients) and E (2 clients) qualify for de minimis exemption.

## Question 6
Pacific Coast Advisers is a federally registered investment adviser with $280 million in AUM serving approximately 220 clients. The firm's Form ADV Part 2A contains the following statement in the "Fees and Compensation" section: "Our standard advisory fee is 1.25% annually on assets under management, billed quarterly in advance. Fees are negotiable, and we may charge more or less than our standard fee depending on the size of the account, complexity of the client's situation, and the services required." Three clients file complaints with the SEC alleging that: (1) Client A, with $5 million in assets, was charged 1.40% annually while being told this was the "standard fee" with no mention that it was negotiable or higher than usual; (2) Client B, with $800,000 in assets, was charged 1.25% and was never informed that fees were negotiable or that larger accounts typically receive discounts; (3) Client C, with $15 million in assets, was charged 1.10% but claims they should have received a greater discount given their account size compared to what other large clients receive. The firm has a practice of charging 1.40%-1.50% for accounts under $1 million, 1.25% for accounts between $1-5 million, 1.00% for accounts between $5-10 million, and 0.85%-0.95% for accounts over $10 million, but this tiered structure is not disclosed anywhere in Form ADV or in client agreements. What violations, if any, has Pacific Coast Advisers committed?

A. The firm violated its disclosure obligations by failing to accurately describe its fee structure in Form ADV Part 2A, and potentially violated anti-fraud provisions by charging Client A above the stated "standard fee" without adequate disclosure
B. The firm has not violated any regulations because Form ADV states fees are negotiable, which provides sufficient disclosure; the specific fee arrangements with individual clients are private contractual matters
C. The firm violated regulations only with respect to Client A, who was charged above the disclosed standard rate; Clients B and C have no valid complaints because they were charged at or below the stated standard fee
D. The firm must immediately reduce all client fees to the lowest rate charged to any client to avoid violating the anti-fraud provisions of the Investment Advisers Act

**Correct Answer:** A. The firm violated its disclosure obligations by failing to accurately describe its fee structure in Form ADV Part 2A, and potentially violated anti-fraud provisions by charging Client A above the stated "standard fee" without adequate disclosure

**Explanation:** Pacific Coast Advisers has committed multiple violations. Form ADV Part 2A must accurately and completely describe the adviser's fee structure. While the brochure mentions that fees are "negotiable," it fails to disclose that the firm actually operates on a tiered fee schedule based on account size, charging significantly different rates (ranging from 0.85% to 1.50%) depending on assets. This omission is material because clients cannot effectively negotiate or understand whether they're being treated fairly without knowing the actual fee structure the firm employs. The statement that "1.25% is our standard fee" is misleading when the firm routinely charges rates from 0.85% to 1.50% - there is no single "standard" fee. Regarding Client A specifically, charging 1.40% while representing this as the "standard fee" (when the brochure states 1.25% is standard) without disclosing it's above the stated standard rate likely violates anti-fraud provisions under Section 206 of the Investment Advisers Act. Even though the brochure says fees are negotiable and "may be more or less," representing 1.40% as the standard fee when the disclosed standard is 1.25% is deceptive. For Client B, while they were charged the stated "standard" rate of 1.25%, the failure to disclose that this rate is actually on the higher end of the firm's range and that accounts of their size typically receive this rate while larger accounts receive substantial discounts may constitute a material omission. Client C's complaint raises issues about fair dealing - if the firm has a practice of charging 0.85%-0.95% for accounts over $10 million, charging Client C (with $15 million) only 1.10% when they could receive 0.85%-0.95% raises questions about whether the fee was truly "negotiated" fairly or whether the client was disadvantaged by inadequate disclosure. The proper remedy is for the firm to amend Form ADV Part 2A to accurately describe its tiered fee structure, provide updated brochures to all clients, potentially refund overcharges, and implement better fee disclosure practices. Option B is incorrect because merely stating fees are "negotiable" is insufficient when the firm has an established fee structure that isn't disclosed. Option C is incorrect because the violations extend beyond just Client A - the inadequate fee disclosure affects all clients' ability to negotiate fairly. Option D is incorrect because charging different fees to different clients is permissible as long as it's properly disclosed; the violation is in the inadequate disclosure, not in having varying fees.

## Question 7
Riverside Investment Management is a state-registered investment adviser with $62 million in AUM. The firm is owned entirely by its founder, Robert Chen, who also serves as the firm's Chief Compliance Officer and only investment adviser representative. Robert is considering various business developments and consults with his attorney about Form ADV filing requirements. The attorney advises him on the following scenarios: (1) Robert is negotiating to sell 40% ownership in Riverside to a private equity firm, which would provide capital for expansion while Robert retains 60% ownership and operational control; (2) Robert is considering hiring his first employee, Susan, who would serve as an administrative assistant handling client correspondence and scheduling but would not provide investment advice or have discretionary authority; (3) Robert has been approached by a local bank that wants to refer wealth management clients to Riverside in exchange for Riverside paying the bank a referral fee of 0.25% of AUM for any referred clients; (4) Robert is planning to change the firm's legal structure from a sole proprietorship to a limited liability company (LLC) for liability protection purposes; (5) Robert's brother-in-law is on the board of directors of a publicly traded technology company, and Robert occasionally recommends this stock to clients based on his own research, though his brother-in-law has never provided him with any inside information. Which of these scenarios would require Form ADV amendments or additional disclosures?

A. Scenarios (1), (3), (4), and (5) require Form ADV amendments or disclosures; scenario (2) does not require any Form ADV changes since Susan would not be providing investment advice
B. All five scenarios require some form of Form ADV amendment or disclosure because each represents a material change in the firm's operations, ownership, conflicts, or structure
C. Only scenarios (1) and (4) require Form ADV amendments because they involve changes to ownership structure; the other scenarios are operational matters that don't require disclosure
D. Scenarios (1), (3), and (4) require amendments; scenarios (2) and (5) do not require disclosure because administrative employees need not be listed and indirect family relationships to recommended securities are too attenuated to constitute conflicts

**Correct Answer:** B. All five scenarios require some form of Form ADV amendment or disclosure because each represents a material change in the firm's operations, ownership, conflicts, or structure

**Explanation:** Each scenario triggers Form ADV requirements: (1) Selling 40% ownership to a private equity firm is a material change in ownership that must be reported in Form ADV Part 1A (Schedule A - Direct Owners and Executive Officers, and Schedule B - Indirect Owners). This represents a significant change in control and ownership structure requiring an "other-than-annual amendment" within 30 days. Even though Robert retains 60% and control, the addition of a 40% owner is material. Additionally, if the private equity firm has any role in operations or imposes any restrictions, this may require additional disclosure in Part 2A. (2) Hiring Susan as the first employee requires updating Form ADV even though she won't provide investment advice. Part 1A asks about the number of employees, and going from zero employees to one employee is a material change. Additionally, if Susan will have access to client information or accounts, her role should be described in the firm's compliance structure. While she may not need to be listed as an IAR or require a Part 2B brochure supplement, the firm's organizational structure has materially changed. (3) The bank referral arrangement absolutely requires disclosure in Form ADV Part 2A under conflicts of interest and referral arrangements. This is a cash solicitation arrangement that must comply with Rule 206(4)-3 of the Investment Advisers Act, which requires: written disclosure to clients of the arrangement, written agreement with the solicitor (bank), and client acknowledgment. The arrangement must be described in Part 2A, including the fee structure and potential conflicts. Additionally, the bank employees making referrals may themselves need to be registered depending on their compensation structure. (4) Changing from a sole proprietorship to an LLC is a fundamental change in legal structure that must be reflected throughout Form ADV Part 1A. This includes changes to: Item 1 (identifying information and legal structure), Item 3 (form of organization), organizational documents, and potentially requires filing an initial application for the LLC while withdrawing the sole proprietorship registration (some states may treat this as a new registration rather than an amendment). This is a material change requiring prompt filing. (5) The brother-in-law's board position at a company Robert recommends creates a conflict of interest that should be disclosed in Form ADV Part 2A. Even though Robert doesn't receive inside information, the family relationship creates a potential conflict - clients might wonder if Robert is recommending the stock because of the family connection rather than purely objective analysis. The relationship creates an appearance of conflict that should be disclosed so clients can make informed decisions. The SEC's position is that conflicts should be disclosed broadly, including indirect relationships that could influence recommendations. Option A is incorrect because scenario (2) does require Form ADV updates regarding employee count and organizational structure. Option C is incorrect because it fails to recognize that scenarios (3) and (5) involve material conflicts and arrangements requiring disclosure, and scenario (2) involves organizational changes. Option D is incorrect because scenario (2) involves material organizational changes (first employee, changing from solo practitioner to having staff), and scenario (5) involves a conflict of interest that, while indirect, should be disclosed given the specific nature of the recommendations and family relationship. The key principle is that Form ADV requires comprehensive disclosure of material changes in ownership, structure, operations, and conflicts of interest, interpreted broadly to ensure investors have complete information.

## Question 8
Metropolitan Wealth Advisors is a federally registered investment adviser with $520 million in AUM serving 340 clients across 12 states. The firm's Form ADV Part 2A states in the "Methods of Analysis, Investment Strategies and Risk of Loss" section: "We employ a diversified investment approach using fundamental analysis of individual securities combined with modern portfolio theory. We invest primarily in publicly traded stocks and bonds, mutual funds, and ETFs. We may also use options for hedging purposes in appropriate client accounts." The compliance department discovers that over the past 18 months, one of the firm's senior portfolio managers, David, has been implementing a highly concentrated investment strategy for approximately 25 of his 60 clients, with these portfolios holding only 8-12 individual stocks (compared to the firm's typical 40-60 holdings) and using leveraged ETFs to amplify returns. Additionally, David has been actively trading options in 15 client accounts, not merely for hedging but as a primary investment strategy to generate income through covered calls and cash-secured puts, with some accounts having 30-40% of their value in options positions. None of these clients were informed that David's approach differed from the firm's stated investment methods, and the Form ADV brochure was not supplemented to describe these strategies. Several of the clients with concentrated, leveraged portfolios have experienced significant volatility, with some accounts declining 25-35% during a recent market correction while the firm's other clients following traditional strategies declined only 12-15%. How should the firm address this situation, and what are the regulatory implications?

A. The firm should immediately restrict David from continuing these strategies, amend Form ADV Part 2A to accurately describe all investment methods actually being employed, provide updated brochures to all clients, and consider whether clients affected by undisclosed strategies are entitled to remediation
B. Since Form ADV mentioned that the firm "may use options" and didn't explicitly prohibit concentrated portfolios, no Form ADV violation occurred; the firm should simply have David provide better performance reports to affected clients explaining the volatility
C. The firm should terminate David for unauthorized trading and report him to regulators, but the firm itself bears no responsibility since David acted independently against firm policy
D. The firm should amend Form ADV to add concentrated portfolios and active options trading as approved strategies, which will retroactively cure any disclosure deficiencies, and no client notification is required since Form ADV is publicly available

**Correct Answer:** A. The firm should immediately restrict David from continuing these strategies, amend Form ADV Part 2A to accurately describe all investment methods actually being employed, provide updated brochures to all clients, and consider whether clients affected by undisclosed strategies are entitled to remediation

**Explanation:** Metropolitan Wealth Advisors faces serious regulatory violations on multiple levels. First, Form ADV Part 2A must accurately describe the investment strategies the firm actually employs, not just those it intends to employ or approves of. The fact that David has been implementing concentrated, leveraged strategies with active options trading for 25-40 clients over 18 months means these are strategies the firm has been employing, whether authorized or not. The Form ADV description of "diversified investment approach" and options "for hedging purposes" is materially misleading when a significant portion of the firm's clients are in highly concentrated portfolios using leveraged ETFs and options for income generation rather than hedging. Second, the firm has violated its fiduciary duty to the affected clients by failing to provide adequate disclosure before implementing strategies that materially differ from what was disclosed in Form ADV and what clients reasonably expected. Clients who engaged the firm based on its stated "diversified approach" have been placed in concentrated, leveraged strategies without informed consent. This likely violates the antifraud provisions of Section 206 of the Investment Advisers Act. Third, the firm's supervision and compliance systems have failed. As a registered investment adviser, Metropolitan is responsible for supervising its IARs and ensuring they operate within disclosed parameters. The fact that David could implement these significant strategy deviations for 18 months without detection represents a failure of the firm's compliance program. Fourth, there are potential suitability issues. While investment advisers don't have formal "suitability" requirements like broker-dealers, they have a fiduciary duty to ensure recommendations and strategies are appropriate for each client's objectives, risk tolerance, and financial situation. Concentrated, leveraged portfolios may be appropriate for some sophisticated, risk-tolerant clients, but implementing them across 25 clients without documented assessment of appropriateness raises serious questions. The firm should take the following steps: (1) Immediately conduct a review of all client accounts David manages to understand the full scope of strategy deviations; (2) Suspend implementation of undisclosed strategies pending review; (3) Amend Form ADV Part 2A to accurately describe all investment strategies actually being employed (or make a definitive decision to prohibit certain strategies and enforce that prohibition); (4) Provide updated brochures to all current clients; (5) Contact affected clients to disclose that strategies employed differed from those disclosed and discuss appropriate next steps; (6) Evaluate whether clients who suffered losses due to undisclosed strategies are entitled to remediation; (7) Strengthen supervision and compliance procedures to prevent future violations; (8) Consider whether voluntary disclosure to regulators is appropriate given the seriousness of the violations. Option B is incorrect because mentioning that the firm "may use options" for hedging does not adequately disclose using options as a primary income-generation strategy with 30-40% of account values in options positions - this is a material difference. Similarly, describing a "diversified approach" is fundamentally inconsistent with concentrated 8-12 stock portfolios. Option C is incorrect because while David may have acted contrary to firm policy, the firm is responsible for supervising its IARs and is liable for their actions within the scope of their employment. The firm cannot disclaim responsibility by blaming a rogue employee - it has supervisory obligations. Option D is incorrect because amending Form ADV does not retroactively cure past disclosure deficiencies or eliminate the firm's obligation to notify affected clients of material changes. Simply making an amended Form ADV publicly available is insufficient - clients must be provided updated brochures and informed of material changes. Additionally, adding these strategies as "approved" without determining whether they should be permitted raises questions about the firm's commitment to its fiduciary obligations.

## Question 11
Northern Lights Investment Counsel is a state-registered investment adviser in Michigan with $68 million in AUM. The firm has clients in Michigan (72 clients), Ohio (8 clients), Indiana (4 clients), Illinois (2 clients), and Wisconsin (1 client). The firm maintains its only office in Michigan. All clients in other states were obtained through referrals from existing clients; the firm has never advertised or solicited business outside of Michigan. The firm is registered with Michigan securities regulators but has never registered in any other state, relying on the de minimis exemption. Last month, the firm's founder, Karen, gave a presentation at a financial planning conference held in Chicago, Illinois, where she spoke about tax-efficient investing strategies. During the presentation, she mentioned her firm's name and showed her contact information on slides. Following the conference, 15 people from various states (including 6 from Illinois, 4 from Indiana, 3 from Ohio, and 2 from Wisconsin) contacted Karen expressing interest in becoming clients. Karen is excited about the potential for growth and wants to accept all 15 as new clients. Her compliance consultant warns her that accepting these clients would have registration implications. Specifically, what are Northern Lights' registration obligations if Karen accepts all 15 new clients?

A. Northern Lights must register in Ohio and Illinois where the combined existing plus new clients would exceed 5, but can continue relying on the de minimis exemption in Indiana and Wisconsin
B. Northern Lights must register in all four states (Ohio, Illinois, Indiana, Wisconsin) because Karen's conference presentation constitutes "holding out" as an investment adviser in Illinois, which vitiates the de minimis exemption in all states
C. Northern Lights need not register in any additional states because all inquiries were unsolicited responses to an educational presentation, preserving the de minimis exemption
D. Northern Lights must register with the SEC rather than individual states because accepting these clients would bring the firm to 102 clients across 6 states, triggering federal registration requirements

**Correct Answer:** B. Northern Lights must register in all four states (Ohio, Illinois, Indiana, Wisconsin) because Karen's conference presentation constitutes "holding out" as an investment adviser in Illinois, which vitiates the de minimis exemption in all states

**Explanation:** The de minimis exemption allows investment advisers to have up to 5 clients in a state during a 12-month period without registering in that state, provided two critical conditions are met: (1) the adviser has no place of business in that state, AND (2) the adviser does not hold itself out generally to the public in that state as an investment adviser. Northern Lights currently satisfies both prongs in Ohio (8 clients but no office and presumably no holding out), Indiana (4 clients), Illinois (2 clients), and Wisconsin (1 client). However, the de minimis exemption is lost even if an adviser has fewer than 5 clients if the adviser "holds out" to the public as an investment adviser in that state. Karen's presentation at a financial planning conference in Chicago where she displayed her firm name and contact information constitutes "holding out" in Illinois. By speaking at a public conference, identifying her firm, and providing contact information, she held herself out as an investment adviser to the public in Illinois. Once the "holding out" prong of the de minimis exemption is violated, the exemption is lost regardless of client count. This means Northern Lights cannot rely on the de minimis exemption in Illinois even if it had zero clients there. Moreover, the "holding out" violation in Illinois has broader implications. Different states and securities attorneys interpret the scope differently, but the most conservative and legally sound interpretation is that once an adviser holds itself out at a public event in one state, attendees from multiple states are exposed to that holding out. The 15 people who contacted Karen came from the conference where she held out - they became aware of Northern Lights specifically because of Karen's public presentation. Therefore, Northern Lights cannot claim it didn't hold out to these prospective clients; they learned of the firm through a public holding-out event. If Northern Lights accepts new clients: Ohio would have 8 + 3 = 11 clients; Illinois would have 2 + 6 = 8 clients; Indiana would have 4 + 4 = 8 clients; Wisconsin would have 1 + 2 = 3 clients. Ohio: 11 clients exceeds the 5-client limit, and the adviser may have lost the de minimis exemption through holding out. Must register. Illinois: 8 clients exceeds 5, AND the adviser held out in Illinois. Must register. Indiana: 8 clients exceeds 5, and arguably the clients resulted from holding out. Must register. Wisconsin: 3 clients is under 5, but if the clients resulted from the holding-out event, the exemption may be lost. The safest course is to register or not accept Wisconsin clients from the conference. Option A is incorrect because it fails to account for the "holding out" violation, which affects the exemption in all states, not just where client counts exceed 5. Option C is incorrect because even though the inquiries were "unsolicited" in the sense that Karen didn't directly solicit these specific individuals, they resulted from her public presentation where she held out as an investment adviser - this vitiates the de minimis exemption. The exemption requires both fewer than 5 clients AND no holding out; violating the holding-out prong defeats the exemption even with unsolicited inquiries. Option D is incorrect regarding SEC registration triggers - having 102 clients across 6 states doesn't trigger federal registration; AUM thresholds do. At $68 million AUM, Northern Lights is well below the $100 million threshold and must remain state-registered. The correct course of action: If Karen wants to accept these clients, Northern Lights should register in Ohio and Illinois immediately (where they'll exceed 5 clients), evaluate registration requirements in Indiana (where they'll exceed 5 clients), and carefully consider whether to register in Wisconsin or decline those prospective clients. Alternatively, Northern Lights could limit how many new clients it accepts from each state to stay under 5 in states where registration is complex or expensive, but even this strategy is risky given the holding-out violation. The lesson: Investment advisers must be extremely cautious about public speaking, advertising, or any activity that could constitute "holding out" in states where they're not registered and are relying on the de minimis exemption. Publishing articles, speaking at conferences, appearing on local media, or similar public activities can vitiate the de minimis exemption even with zero clients in a state.

## Question 12
Sequoia Asset Management is a federally registered investment adviser with $650 million in AUM. The firm's Form ADV Part 2A describes the firm's custody practices as follows: "We do not maintain custody of client funds or securities. All client assets are held at qualified custodians, and clients receive account statements directly from those custodians. We recommend that clients carefully review their custodian statements." However, during an SEC examination, examiners discover several situations that may constitute "custody" under SEC rules: (1) The firm has been given written authorization by 12 clients to deduct advisory fees directly from their accounts at the custodian, with fees being withdrawn quarterly; (2) The firm's managing partner serves as trustee for 8 family trusts that are clients of the firm, giving him legal authority over the trust assets; (3) The firm has standing letters of authorization (SLOAs) from 18 clients authorizing the firm to direct the custodian to transfer funds from the clients' accounts to specified third parties (such as for charitable contributions or distributions to family members); (4) The firm uses a third-party portfolio accounting system that has a "trade-away" arrangement where trades are executed at one broker but settled at the custodian; the firm has login credentials that allow it to view client account information at the custodian and initiate certain administrative actions, though it cannot directly move money; (5) Six clients have given the firm's principals power of attorney over their financial affairs due to the clients' advanced age and health issues, and these POAs allow the principals to sign checks and make withdrawals on behalf of the clients. Based on these facts, does Sequoia actually have custody of client assets, and if so, what are the firm's compliance obligations under the Custody Rule?

A. Sequoia has custody due to situations (1), (2), (3), and (5); the firm must comply with the Custody Rule including obtaining annual surprise examinations by an independent accountant and ensuring clients receive account statements from qualified custodians
B. Sequoia does not have custody because all assets are held at qualified custodians and the firm cannot directly access client funds; the situations described are normal administrative arrangements that don't constitute custody
C. Sequoia has custody only for situations (2) and (5) where personnel have legal authority over client assets; situations (1), (3), and (4) are operational matters that don't trigger custody obligations
D. Sequoia must immediately terminate all arrangements that create custody and revise Form ADV to ensure it has no custody, as federal advisers cannot maintain custody of client assets

**Correct Answer:** A. Sequoia has custody due to situations (1), (2), (3), and (5); the firm must comply with the Custody Rule including obtaining annual surprise examinations by an independent accountant and ensuring clients receive account statements from qualified custodians

**Explanation:** The SEC's Custody Rule (Rule 206(4)-2) defines "custody" broadly to include situations where an adviser holds client funds or securities, or has authority to obtain possession of them. Sequoia's situations trigger custody as follows: (1) Direct fee deduction: Having written authorization to deduct fees directly from client accounts is a form of custody under the rule. While this is a limited form of custody (the adviser can only deduct its own fees, not access funds otherwise), it still constitutes custody. However, there's a specific exception: if the adviser has written authorization from the client, the custodian sends account statements directly to clients, and the adviser has a reasonable belief that clients are receiving statements, the adviser can deduct fees without triggering the full surprise examination requirement. So situation (1) creates custody but may qualify for an exception from some custody requirements if properly structured. (2) Serving as trustee: When the firm's personnel serve as trustees for client trusts, the firm is deemed to have custody of the trust assets. This is one of the most common ways advisers inadvertently obtain custody. The trustee has legal authority over the assets, which constitutes custody under the rule. This absolutely triggers full Custody Rule compliance obligations. (3) Standing Letters of Authorization (SLOAs): This is a developing area of custody rule interpretation. Traditionally, having authority to direct custodians to send client funds to third parties was considered custody. However, the SEC issued a no-action letter (February 2017) providing conditions under which SLOAs don't constitute custody: the client provides written authorization, the transfer is to a specific third party (not the adviser), the custodian verifies the client's instruction, the custodian sends confirmation to the client, the adviser maintains records, and the client can terminate the authorization. If Sequoia's SLOAs meet all these conditions, they may not constitute custody; if they don't meet all conditions, they do constitute custody. Given the facts don't specify whether these conditions are met, situation (3) likely constitutes custody. (4) Login credentials and administrative access: Merely having view-only access or administrative capabilities (like correcting addresses) typically doesn't constitute custody as long as the adviser can't directly move money or securities. Situation (4) likely does not constitute custody if the access is truly limited to viewing and administrative functions. However, if the login credentials allow the firm to initiate transfers or withdrawals (even with custodian approval steps), this could constitute custody. (5) Power of Attorney with financial authority: Having POA that allows signing checks and making withdrawals is definitely custody under the rule. The firm has legal authority to obtain possession of client funds, which is the definition of custody. If Sequoia has custody (which it does through situations 1, 2, 3, and 5), it must comply with the Custody Rule requirements: (a) Client funds and securities must be maintained by a qualified custodian; (b) If the adviser or related persons serve as qualified custodian, clients must receive account statements at least quarterly; (c) If a third party serves as qualified custodian, the custodian must send account statements directly to clients at least quarterly; (d) The adviser must undergo an annual surprise examination by an independent public accountant (with some exceptions, such as the fee deduction exception if properly structured); (e) The adviser must file Form ADV-E annually. There are some exceptions. For situation (1) (fee deduction), if Sequoia has proper written authorization, the custodian sends statements, and Sequoia has reasonable belief clients receive them, the surprise examination requirement may not apply to these accounts. For situation (2) (trustee), if the trustee is subject to regular audit by an independent accountant, the surprise exam requirement may not apply. However, Sequoia has still been deficient by stating in Form ADV that it doesn't have custody when in fact it does. The firm must: (1) Amend Form ADV to accurately disclose that it has custody; (2) Review each custody situation to determine applicable compliance obligations; (3) Implement surprise examination by independent accountant (unless exceptions apply); (4) Verify clients receive account statements from custodians; (5) File Form ADV-E annually; (6) Implement controls to track and monitor all situations that may create custody. Option B is incorrect because custody is defined by authority over assets, not just physical possession. Many of Sequoia's arrangements constitute custody even though assets are at third-party custodians. Option C is incorrect because situations (1) and (3) also trigger custody (though (1) may qualify for exceptions). Option D is incorrect because federal advisers can maintain custody; they just must comply with the Custody Rule requirements. There's no prohibition on advisers having custody - it simply triggers specific compliance obligations.

While some flexibility exists for occasional exceptions (situations 1 and 2), material new services (situations 3, 4, and 5) require disclosure. Option C is too strict - it would require Form ADV amendments every time a single client outside normal parameters is accepted, which is impractical and unnecessary. Firms can have target client profiles while occasionally accepting outliers, as long as the exceptions don't become the rule. Option D is incorrect in asserting that accepting clients below stated minimums violates "requirements" - minimums are the firm's own policy, not regulatory requirements, and can be waived with documentation. However, D is correct that situations 3, 4, and 5 require amendments. The key principle: Form ADV must accurately describe the firm's business practices, but "accurately describe" doesn't mean "describe with perfect precision every possible variation." Firms can state they "primarily" serve a type of client while occasionally serving others. They can state account minimums while sometimes waiving them. However, when firms add materially new services (tax prep, hourly consulting) or materially new client types (retirement plans), these changes must be reflected in Form ADV. The test is: Would a reasonable client or prospective client be misled by the current Form ADV description given the firm's actual practices? If yes, amendment is required.

## Question 18

**Correct Answer:** A. The firm must immediately amend Form ADV to disclose custody, engage an independent public accountant to conduct a surprise examination of client accounts, ensure clients receive quarterly statements from custodians, and file Form ADV-E annually

**Explanation:** Summit Financial Advisory has custody of client assets based on multiple situations, making its current Form ADV disclosure false and creating serious Custody Rule compliance obligations. Let's analyze each situation: (1) Direct fee deduction with written authorization: Having written authorization to deduct advisory fees directly from client accounts is a form of custody under Rule 206(4)-2. However, there's an exception: if the adviser has written client authorization, the qualified custodian sends account statements directly to clients at least quarterly, and the adviser has a reasonable basis to believe clients are receiving statements, the surprise examination requirement does not apply to these accounts. So situation (1) creates custody but may qualify for a surprise examination exception. (2) Serving as executor of deceased client's estate: When firm personnel serve as executor/personal representative for a client estate and the estate assets are managed by the firm, the firm has custody. (3) Serving as trustee: When firm personnel serve as trustees for client trusts, the firm has custody because the trustee has legal authority over trust assets. This is one of the most common ways advisers inadvertently obtain custody. This creates custody requiring full Custody Rule compliance. However, there's an exception: if the trustee is subject to regular examination by a federal or state regulatory authority or an independent public accountant, surprise examination may not be required. For most family trusts, this exception doesn't apply, so full surprise examination is required. This creates custody requiring full Custody Rule compliance.
